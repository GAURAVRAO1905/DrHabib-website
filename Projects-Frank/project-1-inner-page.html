Quantitative phase imaging of acoustically trapped biological cells

For last several years, a wide range of acoustic-based microparticle trapping and manipulation functions (e.g., sorting, focusing, mixing, and patterning) has been employed. None of these approaches, however, have achieved the dexterity of optical tweezers. The acoustic based trapping/manipulations techniques do not provide quantitative information about the morphological changes in the trapped or manipulated microparticles or the biological objects. Therefore, the main aim of the project is to integrate optical based quantitative phase imaging (QPI) with the acoustic trapping for the biological specimen’s. QPI is a powerful method for measuring different optical parameters associated with biological objects, such as, cell dynamics, which depends on thickness and refractive index fluctuation. It can provide the quantitative information with higher accuracy of the biological specimen’s. On the other hand, conventional IDTs for trapping or manipulation of cells are made of piezoelectric LiNbO3 single crystal, which are non-flexible, expensive.

High frequency ultrasonic transducer array for acoustically trapped photoacoustic imaging

The central part of the project will be to open a new avenue in the field of acoustically trapped in vivo imaging by employing PAM for biological samples. Thick biological samples are difficult to penetrate and too dense to isolate the relevant information without introducing aberrations. This is the key technological challenge. Microscopes that support 3D sectioning in thick samples require special consideration of the physical models of the microscopes, the optical properties of the sample, and the statistical distribution of the contribution of out-of-focus light in order to perform further resolution enhancement. Moreover, a combination and optics and acoustic centric approach is needed to enhance the penetration depth as well as resolution. Acoustic or photoacoustic modality supports excellent penetration into thick samples without compromising the resolution.
